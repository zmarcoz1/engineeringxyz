Machine flow

3 places throughput leaks out of a prototype station before the process even starts.

1. The part-entry state is never defined tightly enough.

If the part can arrive in multiple orientations or ambiguous positions, the machine starts every cycle by inheriting avoidable uncertainty.

2. Transfer timing is designed locally instead of across the full station sequence.

An actuator can move correctly and still create drag if the handoff between loading, motion, process, and discharge is not treated as one machine rhythm.

3. Reset and recovery states are ignored until startup.

Stations lose more time in awkward restart behavior than teams expect. Good machine concepts make the ready state obvious and recovery predictable.

Fixture design

3 common fixture mistakes that appear only after operator use begins.

1. The part locates correctly, but loads awkwardly.

A fixture can be dimensionally correct and still fail in practice if the operator has to fight access, visibility, or orientation every cycle.

2. Changeover assumptions are too optimistic.

What looks like a quick part-family swap in CAD can become a slow station reset if clamps, nests, or adjustment points are not designed for repeated use.

3. Variation is treated as an exception instead of a design condition.

Real stations see tolerance spread, wear, and inconsistent human handling. Fixture strategy has to account for those realities from the beginning.

Guarding and reset

Where confirmation, guarding, and reset logic belong in production hardware.

1. At the point of part entry

The easiest mistake to prevent is often the earliest one: wrong orientation, incomplete seating, or incorrect setup before the cycle even begins.

2. At the confirmation point before critical motion or process

Before pressing, scanning, coating, or transfer motion, the station should make the correct condition more likely than the incorrect one.

3. In the reset state after each cycle

A station that resets ambiguously invites the next error. Good tooling design makes the ready state visually and mechanically obvious.
